Voltage

Turn on the faucet in your kitchen: the force that's pushing out the water is pressure. And by adjusting the mixing tap you can adjust that pressure to your needs to push out more - or less - water per second. Voltage is like that, just with pushing electricity. Your components need a certain amount of electricity to do their job. More work per second - more voltage per second. You don't want to 'starve' your components. They won't work. Imagine trying to run a car engine on a drop of gas - it's just not enough to run it. And the bigger the engine - and its power output - the more gas it needs to work. The faster the CPU (and its output) - the more voltage it needs to work. The faster the GPU (and its output) - the more voltage it needs to work. The faster the RAM (and its output) - the more voltage it needs to work. Too little voltage to run the demand - bad things happen. The best - BSOD or some sort of reboot. That's usually a starved CPU. The worst - you permanently corrupt your files - they will never open. Or even worse - you corrupt a system file and can't boot anymore. Literally have to reinstall the whole system. This is usually a starved RAM problem. (Don't starve your RAM kids) Too much voltage to run the demand - bad things happen. A little too much - just makes your stuff hotter. And degrade faster. A not-little too much - just going to fry it. RIP. It's always a balance between stability, temperature and performance. Pick 2.
