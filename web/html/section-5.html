Some heat math

If you only demand 2.4 GHz performance out of your CPU, then the voltage needed to sustain this level of demand is much lower than if you were to demand 4.1 GHz from the same CPU. The higher the demand - the higher voltage should be. The lower the demand, the lower the voltage can be. Now. Higher frequency (MHz) makes it run hotter. Because there's more work being done. And higher voltage (v) - which is required to run at higher performance - makes it even hotter. These two stack on top each other like multipliers. Frequency is simple. A CPU that runs at 4.0 GHz produces twice as much heat as if were running at 2.0 GHz. Because it's twice faster. 100% increase in frequency = 100% increase in heat. It's a direct, linear increase. Very simple. Now, not only 4.0 GHz requires more voltage than 2.0 GHz the amount of heat from that voltage is not scaled linearly. But quadratically. (yep, you need 5th grade math to read the next 5 lines) -> A real world scenario: You bump voltage from 1.1v to 1.2v, 1.1² = 1.21 1.2² = 1.44 1.44/1.21 = 19% more You bumped voltage by 9% and got 19% more heat. And for what? To run at hotter frequencies to begin with? Ooph. The synergy between Frequency (MHz) and Voltage (V) ramps up fast.
