RAM - overclocking, preparation

Overclocking RAM to its absolute potential is a full blown hobby. A black hole to waste you time into. No reasonable person should ever bother. But all progress depends on being unreasonable. There is a lot that goes into this process. It's much more complicated and technical. Think of RAM as server for your CPU. In CPU intensive tasks, you can get a noticeable performance boost from RAM OC. And that's on top of CPU overclocking. You can also brick your files and/or operating system - which is files - permanently. Before you start fiddling with RAM you should prepare a couple of things. First, find out how to reset BIOS/Clear CMOS. On most laptops you can hold the power button for 60 seconds to reset it. But not always. Either way, you need to figure out how to reset BIOS. Every step a little-too-aggressive bricks your boot process. Because things have to load into RAM to boot. And if RAM fails, things can't load into RAM - so you can't boot. You won't even get to see your BIOS. Be aware that when you reset BIOS - you reset all the BIOS changes you made, such as disk priority or voltage unlocks. Second, check Safe Boot in BIOS. This is a BIOS setting that automatically resets BIOS after multiple failed boots. Helpful. But not guaranteed to work, that's why I put it second. Third, CPU / GPU stress testing provides immediate feedback. RAM? Not always. If you went way too aggressive - you just won't boot. But if you went mildly too aggressive - you will boot. And RAM is going to corrupt all your files as it processes them in the background. So please. You have to test with Memtest / OCCT / TM5 at every step up. At least for 30 minutes. These are not your final tests, but you can get a feel whether RAM is throwing errors right off the bat. This is omega important. Much more important that catching a BSOD or reboots and here's why. Think about how RAM works - it loads stuff from your hard drive for your CPU to use. When RAM completes processing that data - it unloads it back to the hard drive. A faulty or 'starved' RAM makes errors while managing that data. The data now has errors - it has become corrupted. Then RAM unloads that corrupted data onto your hard drive, overwriting the files. It doesn't know the data is corrupted. It just loads and unloads it. It got the original file, fucked it up, and returned it. And now you have a permanently damaged file on your hard drive. This is pretty bad as is - because you might not be able to open or use that file anymore. Now imagine that was a system file. It's game over. And guess where you operating system is - always - by default? That's right - in RAM. So even if you successfully booted into operating system, it's not at all means the RAM is not producing errors right now. That's why you absolutely have to check RAM health at every step. Your 'final' RAM test should be hours long. We are talking 100 cycles long. That's going to confirm and finalize it.
